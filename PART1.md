# 1. Introduction

Nuclear science began with one of the greatest accidental discoveries in the history of physics—the discovery of **radioactivity**.

Before 1896, scientists believed that atoms were the smallest indivisible building blocks of matter. The atom was considered perfectly stable and incapable of changing into another element.

The discovery of radioactivity completely changed this belief.

Scientists realized that:

- Atoms are not indivisible.
- Atoms possess internal structure.
- Some atoms are unstable.
- Unstable atoms spontaneously emit energy and particles.
- One element can transform into another through radioactive decay.

This discovery eventually led to:

- Nuclear Physics
- Nuclear Chemistry
- Nuclear Medicine
- Nuclear Engineering
- Nuclear Reactors
- Nuclear Power Plants
- Nuclear Weapons

Without the discovery of radioactivity, none of today's nuclear technologies would exist.

# 2. The World Before Radioactivity

During the late nineteenth century, the scientific understanding of matter was very limited.

Scientists believed that:

- Atoms were solid, indivisible particles.
- Chemical elements could not transform into one another.
- Energy came mainly from heat, electricity, or chemical reactions.
- The internal structure of atoms was unknown.

The periodic table had already been developed by Dmitri Mendeleev, but scientists still had no idea what existed inside an atom.

Everything changed in the year 1895.

---

# 3. Discovery of X-rays (1895)

The first step toward nuclear science occurred in 1895.

German physicist **Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen** was performing experiments with cathode ray tubes.

During one experiment he noticed that a fluorescent screen located several meters away began glowing even though the tube was completely covered.

He concluded that an unknown type of invisible radiation was being produced.

Since its nature was unknown, he called it:

> **X-rays**

The discovery immediately attracted worldwide attention because X-rays could pass through many materials and reveal the internal structure of objects.

Within months, physicians began using X-rays to image broken bones.

The discovery of X-rays inspired scientists across Europe to investigate whether other invisible radiations might also exist.

# 4. Discovery of Radioactivity (1896)

French physicist **Henri Becquerel** became interested in studying materials that fluoresced after exposure to sunlight.

He wondered:

> "Do fluorescent materials emit X-rays after absorbing sunlight?"

To investigate this idea, he placed uranium salts on top of photographic plates wrapped in black paper.

He expected:

Sunlight

↓

Uranium absorbs sunlight

↓

Uranium emits X-rays

↓

Photographic plate becomes exposed

However, bad weather interrupted his experiment.

The uranium samples remained inside a dark drawer for several days.

When Becquerel later developed the photographic plates, he observed something remarkable.

The plates had become strongly exposed even though:

- No sunlight had reached the uranium.
- No visible light was present.
- No external energy source was available.

He realized that uranium was emitting invisible radiation continuously on its own.

This phenomenon became known as **radioactivity**.

This accidental discovery marks the birth of nuclear science.

---

# 5. Henri Becquerel (1852–1908)

Henri Becquerel was a French physicist from a family of distinguished scientists.

His accidental discovery in 1896 demonstrated that certain elements naturally emit radiation without any external energy source.

His work showed for the first time that atoms are not perfectly stable.

For this discovery, Becquerel shared the **1903 Nobel Prize in Physics** with Marie Curie and Pierre Curie.

---

# 6. Marie Curie and Pierre Curie

After Becquerel's discovery, Marie Curie decided to investigate radioactive materials in greater detail.

She introduced the term:

> **Radioactivity**

Marie Curie carefully measured the radiation emitted by different elements.

She discovered that:

- Uranium compounds were radioactive.
- Thorium was also radioactive.
- Some minerals emitted much more radiation than pure uranium.

This suggested that unknown radioactive elements were present.

Together with Pierre Curie, she began an enormous effort to isolate these unknown elements.

They processed several tons of uranium ore to extract tiny amounts of new radioactive substances.

# 7. Discovery of Polonium and Radium

In 1898, the Curies discovered two entirely new elements.

## Polonium (Po)

Named after Marie Curie's homeland, Poland.

Properties:

- Highly radioactive
- Rare in nature
- Alpha emitter

---

## Radium (Ra)

Radium proved to be one of the most radioactive elements ever discovered.

It continuously emitted:

- Heat
- Light
- Radiation

without any external energy source.

This observation suggested that enormous amounts of energy were stored inside atoms.

Many years later, Einstein explained this using:

\[
E = mc^2
\]

# 8. Understanding Radioactive Decay

Scientists eventually realized that radioactive atoms are unstable.

An unstable nucleus naturally transforms into a more stable configuration by emitting radiation.

This spontaneous transformation is called:

> **Radioactive Decay**

Important characteristics:

- Completely spontaneous
- Cannot be stopped
- Cannot be accelerated by temperature
- Cannot be accelerated by pressure
- Independent of chemical reactions

Each radioactive nucleus behaves randomly.

However, when a very large number of atoms are considered together, their behavior follows precise statistical laws.

---

# 9. Types of Radioactive Radiation

Further experiments by Ernest Rutherford showed that radioactive emissions consist of three different types.

## Alpha (α) Radiation

- Helium nucleus
- Charge = +2
- Heavy particle
- Low penetration
- High ionization

Can be stopped by:

- Paper
- Skin

---

## Beta (β) Radiation

- High-speed electron or positron
- Charge = ±1
- Medium penetration
- Medium ionization

Can be stopped by:

- Aluminum sheet

---

## Gamma (γ) Radiation

- Electromagnetic wave
- No mass
- No charge
- Extremely penetrating
- Low ionization

Requires:

- Thick lead
- Thick concrete

for shielding.

---

# 10. Why Radioactivity Occurs

Not every nucleus is stable.

A nucleus contains:

- Protons
- Neutrons

Two opposing forces act inside the nucleus.

### Attractive Force

Strong Nuclear Force

- Very strong
- Very short range
- Holds nucleons together

### Repulsive Force

Electrostatic Force

- Acts between protons
- Pushes protons apart

If these forces are not properly balanced,

the nucleus becomes unstable and undergoes radioactive decay.

This is the origin of natural radioactivity.

# 11. Early Applications of Radioactivity

Within only a few years of its discovery, radioactivity found many applications.

Examples include:

- Medical diagnosis
- Cancer treatment
- Scientific research
- Geological dating
- Industrial radiography
- Material characterization

Unfortunately, during the early years, scientists were unaware of the biological dangers of radiation.

Many researchers, including Marie Curie, suffered severe health effects after prolonged exposure.

Today, strict radiation safety procedures are followed worldwide.

---

# 12. Impact on Modern Science

The discovery of radioactivity completely transformed science.

It led directly to the development of:

- Nuclear Physics
- Atomic Structure
- Quantum Mechanics
- Nuclear Chemistry
- Nuclear Engineering
- Reactor Physics
- Nuclear Medicine
- Radiation Therapy
- Nuclear Power
- Generation-IV Nuclear Reactor Technology

Every modern nuclear reactor ultimately traces its origins back to Becquerel's accidental experiment in 1896.

# 14. Key Takeaways

- Radioactivity was discovered accidentally by Henri Becquerel in 1896.
- Marie Curie introduced the term "radioactivity."
- Radioactive atoms emit energy spontaneously.
- Radioactivity revealed that atoms are not indivisible.
- Alpha, beta, and gamma radiation have different properties.
- Nuclear stability depends on the balance between the strong nuclear force and electrostatic repulsion.
- The discovery of radioactivity laid the foundation for all modern nuclear science and engineering.

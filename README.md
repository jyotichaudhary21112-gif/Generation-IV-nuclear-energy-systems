# Nuclear Proliferation

## Overview

Nuclear proliferation refers to the spread of nuclear weapons or the materials and technologies required to manufacture them. While nuclear power plants are designed for peaceful electricity generation, some materials produced during the nuclear fuel cycle, particularly plutonium-239 (Pu-239), can potentially be used in nuclear weapons if diverted from civilian use. Therefore, preventing nuclear proliferation is a major objective of modern nuclear energy programs.

---

## Why is Nuclear Proliferation a Concern?

Commercial nuclear reactors produce electricity through controlled nuclear fission, but they also generate fissile materials during reactor operation. The primary concern is that these materials could be chemically separated from spent nuclear fuel and misused for weapons production.

The risk is associated with:
- Production of plutonium during reactor operation.
- Separation of plutonium during spent fuel reprocessing.
- Unauthorized diversion of fissile materials.

---

## Fissile and Fertile Materials

### Fissile Materials
Fissile materials can sustain a nuclear chain reaction with slow (thermal) neutrons.

Examples:
- Uranium-235 (U-235)
- Plutonium-239 (Pu-239)
- Plutonium-241 (Pu-241)
- Uranium-233 (U-233)

### Fertile Materials
Fertile materials cannot undergo fission directly with thermal neutrons but can be converted into fissile materials after neutron absorption.

Examples:
- Uranium-238 → Plutonium-239
- Thorium-232 → Uranium-233

---

## Production of Plutonium in Nuclear Reactors

Inside a reactor, uranium-238 absorbs neutrons and is converted into plutonium-239 through radioactive decay.

```text
U-238
   │ + neutron
   ▼
U-239
   ▼ β-
Np-239
   ▼ β-
Pu-239
```

This process occurs naturally during reactor operation.

## Critical Mass

Critical mass is the **minimum amount of fissile material required to sustain a self-supporting nuclear chain reaction**.

- Below critical mass → chain reaction stops.
- At critical mass → steady chain reaction.
- Above critical mass → chain reaction increases rapidly.

Commercial nuclear reactors are carefully designed to operate at controlled criticality, whereas nuclear weapons require a rapidly supercritical configuration.

Approximate critical masses reported in the reference document:

| Material | Critical Mass |
|-----------|--------------:|
| U-235 (93% enriched) | 17 kg |
| U-235 (20% enriched) | 250 kg |
| U-235 (10% enriched) | 1000 kg |
| U-235 (≤5% enriched) | Not practical for weapons |
| Pu-239 (95%) | 4 kg |

Higher enrichment results in a smaller critical mass because more fissile atoms are available to sustain the chain reaction.

---

## Special Nuclear Materials (SNM)

Special Nuclear Materials (SNM) are materials capable of sustaining a nuclear chain reaction.

They include:
- Highly enriched uranium (U-235)
- Plutonium (Pu-239, Pu-241)

These materials require strict international safeguards because of their potential use in nuclear weapons.

---

## Fuel Reprocessing and Proliferation

Spent nuclear fuel still contains valuable uranium and plutonium that can be recycled.

Advantages of reprocessing include:
- Reduced high-level waste volume.
- Recovery of usable fuel.
- Reduced uranium mining requirements.

However, reprocessing also increases proliferation concerns because plutonium is chemically separated from spent fuel.

---

## PUREX Process

PUREX (Plutonium Uranium Redox Extraction) is the conventional spent fuel reprocessing method.

It separates:
- Uranium
- Plutonium
- High-level radioactive waste

The separated plutonium stream must be carefully safeguarded to prevent diversion.

---

## COEX Process

COEX (Co-Extraction) is an improved reprocessing technology.

Instead of separating plutonium alone, COEX keeps uranium and plutonium mixed together.

This mixed product can still be recycled as reactor fuel but is much less suitable for weapons applications, thereby improving proliferation resistance.

---

## Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)

The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), established in **1970**, aims to:

- Prevent the spread of nuclear weapons.
- Promote peaceful uses of nuclear energy.
- Encourage nuclear disarmament.

Countries using civilian nuclear technology agree to international inspections to verify that nuclear materials are not diverted for military purposes.

---

## IAEA Safeguards

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) monitors civilian nuclear facilities through:

- Material accounting
- Facility inspections
- Physical security
- Containment and surveillance
- Verification of nuclear material inventories

These safeguards help ensure that fissile materials remain in peaceful civilian use.

---

## Proliferation Resistance in Generation IV Reactors

One of the primary goals of Generation IV nuclear reactors is to reduce proliferation risk by:

- Minimizing production of weapons-usable materials.
- Developing proliferation-resistant fuel cycles.
- Improving physical protection and safeguards.
- Reducing access to separated plutonium.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Nuclear proliferation is the spread of nuclear weapons or weapons-usable materials.
- The primary proliferation concern in nuclear power is the production and separation of plutonium.
- Critical mass is the minimum quantity of fissile material required to sustain a chain reaction.
- Fuel reprocessing offers significant resource benefits but must be carefully safeguarded.
- PUREX separates plutonium, whereas COEX keeps uranium and plutonium together to reduce proliferation risk.
- International agreements such as the **NPT** and monitoring by the **IAEA** play a vital role in preventing the misuse of civilian nuclear technology.
- Modern Generation IV reactor concepts incorporate proliferation resistance as a fundamental design objective.

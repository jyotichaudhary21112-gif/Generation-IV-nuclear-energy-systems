# 1. Introduction

By the early 1930s, scientists understood that:

- Atoms contain a nucleus.
- The nucleus contains protons and neutrons.
- Radioactive nuclei emit particles spontaneously.

However, one important question remained unanswered:

> **Can humans deliberately change the nucleus of an atom and release its stored energy?**

The answer came through the discovery of **nuclear fission**, one of the most important events in scientific history.

This discovery eventually led to:

- Nuclear reactors
- Nuclear power plants
- Nuclear submarines
- Nuclear weapons
- Generation-IV reactor technology

---

# 2. The Search for Artificial Nuclear Reactions

After James Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932, scientists quickly realized that neutrons could easily penetrate atomic nuclei.

Unlike positively charged particles, neutrons have **no electric charge**, so they are not repelled by the positively charged nucleus.

Scientists around the world began bombarding heavy elements with neutrons to study how nuclei responded.

One of these heavy elements was **uranium**, the heaviest naturally occurring element.

No one expected what would happen next.

---

# 3. Discovery of Nuclear Fission (1938)

In 1938, German scientists:

- **Otto Hahn**
- **Fritz Strassmann**

bombarded uranium with slow neutrons.

Instead of producing slightly heavier elements as expected, they detected **barium**, an element with roughly half the mass of uranium.

This result was completely unexpected.

They could not explain how uranium had transformed into a much lighter element.

---

## Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch

The explanation came from Austrian physicist **Lise Meitner** and her nephew **Otto Frisch**.

They proposed that the uranium nucleus had **split into two smaller nuclei**.

Frisch named this process:

> **Nuclear Fission**

The word *fission* was borrowed from biology, where it describes the division of a cell into two smaller cells.

---

# 4. What is Nuclear Fission?

Nuclear fission is the process in which a **heavy atomic nucleus splits into two or more lighter nuclei**, releasing:

- A large amount of energy
- Additional neutrons
- Gamma radiation

A typical fission reaction is

```text
Neutron

↓

Uranium-235

↓

Excited Uranium-236

↓

Fission Fragments

+

2–3 Neutrons

+

~200 MeV Energy
```

Unlike chemical reactions, where energy comes from electron rearrangement, nuclear fission releases energy from the atomic nucleus.

---

# 5. Why Uranium-235 Undergoes Fission

Uranium-235 is one of the few naturally occurring **fissile materials**.

When it absorbs a slow neutron:

```text
²³⁵U + n

↓

²³⁶U*
```

the nucleus becomes highly unstable.

Instead of remaining intact, it stretches and eventually splits into two smaller nuclei.

This occurs because:

- Electrostatic repulsion between protons increases.
- The strong nuclear force can no longer hold the elongated nucleus together.

The nucleus finally breaks apart.

---

# 6. Energy Released During Fission

Each fission event releases approximately:

```text
≈ 200 MeV
```

of energy.

This energy appears as:

- Kinetic energy of the fission fragments
- Kinetic energy of emitted neutrons
- Gamma radiation
- Beta decay of fission products

The energy originates from the **mass defect**, explained by Einstein's equation:

```text
E = mc²
```

A tiny amount of mass is converted directly into energy.

Although the mass loss is extremely small, the speed of light squared (\(c^2\)) is enormous, resulting in a tremendous energy release.

---

# 7. Discovery of the Chain Reaction

One of the most remarkable features of nuclear fission is that it releases **additional neutrons**.

These neutrons can induce fission in other uranium nuclei.

This creates a repeating process:

```text
Fission

↓

2–3 Neutrons Released

↓

More Uranium Nuclei Undergo Fission

↓

More Neutrons Released

↓

More Fission
```

This self-sustaining process is called a **chain reaction**.

---

# 8. Controlled vs Uncontrolled Chain Reactions

The chain reaction can proceed in two very different ways.

## Controlled Chain Reaction

- Number of fissions remains constant.
- Power output is stable.
- Used in nuclear reactors.

```text
One fission

↓

One new fission
```

---

## Uncontrolled Chain Reaction

- Number of fissions increases rapidly.
- Energy is released almost instantaneously.
- Used in nuclear weapons.

```text
1

↓

2

↓

4

↓

8

↓

16

↓

32
```

The key challenge in reactor engineering is maintaining a **controlled chain reaction**.

---

# 9. Enrico Fermi

Italian physicist **Enrico Fermi** played the central role in developing the first nuclear reactor.

His major contributions include:

- Discovery that slow neutrons are more effective at inducing fission.
- Development of neutron moderation.
- Design of the first controlled chain reaction.
- Establishment of reactor physics.

Fermi is widely regarded as the **father of the nuclear reactor**.

---

# 10. Chicago Pile-1 (CP-1)

The world's first nuclear reactor was called:

> **Chicago Pile-1 (CP-1)**

Location:

- University of Chicago
- Stagg Field squash court

Date:

> **December 2, 1942**

Purpose:

To demonstrate that a **controlled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction** was possible.

---

# 11. Construction of the First Nuclear Reactor

Chicago Pile-1 was surprisingly simple.

It consisted of:

- Graphite blocks
- Uranium metal
- Uranium oxide
- Cadmium control rods
- Wooden support structure

The graphite acted as a **neutron moderator**, slowing down fast neutrons to increase the probability of fission.

Cadmium rods absorbed neutrons and controlled the reaction.

No electricity was generated.

The reactor was built solely to prove that a controlled chain reaction could be sustained.

---

# 12. The First Controlled Chain Reaction

On **December 2, 1942**, Enrico Fermi and his team gradually withdrew the control rods.

As more neutrons became available:

- The neutron population increased.
- The fission rate increased.
- Eventually, the reactor reached **criticality**.

For the first time in human history:

> A controlled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction was achieved.

After operating for about 28 minutes, the reactor was safely shut down by reinserting the control rods.

This marked the beginning of the nuclear age.

---

# 13. Basic Components of a Nuclear Reactor

Chicago Pile-1 introduced the essential components found in modern reactors.

## Fuel

Provides fissile material.

Examples:

- Uranium-235
- Plutonium-239

---

## Moderator

Slows down fast neutrons.

Examples:

- Graphite
- Light Water
- Heavy Water

---

## Control Rods

Absorb neutrons to regulate the chain reaction.

Materials include:

- Cadmium
- Boron
- Hafnium

---

## Reflector

Returns escaping neutrons back into the core, improving neutron economy.

---

# 14. Reactor Criticality

The behavior of a reactor is described by the **effective multiplication factor** (\(k_{eff}\)).

### Subcritical

```text
k < 1
```

More neutrons are lost than produced.

The chain reaction dies out.

---

### Critical

```text
k = 1
```

Each fission causes exactly one more fission.

The reactor operates at steady power.

---

### Supercritical

```text
k > 1
```

Each fission produces more than one new fission.

Power increases.

In reactors, this increase is carefully controlled.

---

# 15. Why Chicago Pile-1 Changed History

Chicago Pile-1 demonstrated that nuclear energy could be controlled for peaceful purposes.

It became the foundation for:

- Commercial nuclear power plants
- Research reactors
- Naval propulsion systems
- Radioisotope production
- Generation-I reactors
- Generation-II reactors
- Generation-III reactors
- Generation-IV reactor concepts

Every modern nuclear reactor can trace its origin back to Chicago Pile-1.

# 17. Key Takeaways

- Nuclear fission was discovered experimentally by Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann.
- Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch correctly explained the phenomenon and coined the term **fission**.
- Uranium-235 is a fissile isotope that can sustain a chain reaction.
- Each fission event releases approximately **200 MeV** of energy and 2–3 neutrons.
- A chain reaction forms because emitted neutrons trigger additional fission events.
- Enrico Fermi led the development of the world's first nuclear reactor.
- Chicago Pile-1 achieved the first controlled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction on **December 2, 1942**.
- The concepts of **fuel**, **moderator**, **control rods**, and **criticality** established in CP-1 remain fundamental to modern reactor design.

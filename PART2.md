# 1. Introduction

The discovery of radioactivity raised an important question:

> **What is inside an atom?**

Scientists had already learned that atoms could emit radiation, but they still did not understand the internal structure responsible for this phenomenon.

Between **1897 and 1932**, a series of remarkable discoveries transformed our understanding of matter.

Scientists discovered:

- The electron
- The atomic nucleus
- The proton
- The neutron
- Nuclear binding energy
- Mass-energy equivalence

These discoveries laid the scientific foundation for:

- Nuclear Physics
- Nuclear Fission
- Nuclear Reactors
- Nuclear Power Generation

---

# 2. The Atom Before Modern Physics

For nearly 2000 years, the atom was believed to be the smallest indivisible particle of matter.

John Dalton's Atomic Theory (1803) proposed that:

- Matter consists of atoms.
- Atoms cannot be divided.
- Atoms of different elements are different.
- Chemical reactions involve rearrangement of atoms.

Although this theory successfully explained chemical reactions, it could not explain:

- Radioactivity
- Electricity
- Magnetism
- Atomic spectra

Scientists began searching for smaller particles inside the atom.

---

# 3. Discovery of the Electron (1897)

The first subatomic particle to be discovered was the **electron**.

British physicist **J. J. Thomson** studied cathode rays using a discharge tube.

By applying electric and magnetic fields, he observed that the rays were deflected.

This proved that the rays consisted of negatively charged particles.

These particles were later named **electrons**.

### Properties of the Electron

| Property | Value |
|----------|--------|
| Symbol | e⁻ |
| Charge | -1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C |
| Mass | 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg |

The discovery of the electron proved that atoms are **not indivisible**.

---

# 4. Thomson's Plum Pudding Model

After discovering the electron, Thomson proposed a new model of the atom.

According to this model:

- The atom is a positively charged sphere.
- Electrons are embedded throughout the sphere.
- The total positive charge balances the negative charge of the electrons.

This became known as the **Plum Pudding Model**.

Although it explained electrical neutrality, it failed to explain experimental observations.

---

# 5. Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment (1909)

One of the most important experiments in physics was performed by:

- Ernest Rutherford
- Hans Geiger
- Ernest Marsden

### Experimental Setup

- A beam of alpha particles was directed at an extremely thin gold foil.
- A fluorescent screen detected the scattered alpha particles.

### Expected Result

According to Thomson's model:

- Alpha particles should pass almost straight through the foil.

### Actual Observation

Scientists observed:

- Most alpha particles passed straight through.
- Some were slightly deflected.
- A very small number were scattered backward.

This result completely contradicted Thomson's atomic model.

---

# 6. Discovery of the Atomic Nucleus

From the scattering experiment, Rutherford concluded:

- Most of the atom is empty space.
- Almost all the mass is concentrated in a tiny central region.
- This central region carries a positive charge.

He called this region the **atomic nucleus**.

This was one of the greatest discoveries in modern science.

---

# 7. Rutherford's Nuclear Model

According to Rutherford:

- The nucleus is extremely small.
- The nucleus contains nearly all the mass of the atom.
- Electrons revolve around the nucleus.
- Most of the atom is empty space.

Although this model explained the scattering experiment, it had one major problem.

According to classical electromagnetism, accelerating electrons should continuously emit radiation and spiral into the nucleus.

Therefore, the Rutherford model predicted that atoms should be unstable.

This contradiction was solved by Niels Bohr.

---

# 8. Bohr's Atomic Model (1913)

Niels Bohr introduced the concept of **quantized electron orbits**.

According to Bohr:

- Electrons occupy specific energy levels.
- Electrons do not radiate energy while moving in allowed orbits.
- Radiation is emitted only when electrons jump between energy levels.

This model successfully explained:

- Hydrogen emission spectrum
- Atomic stability

Although later replaced by quantum mechanics, Bohr's model remains important for understanding atomic structure.

---

# 9. Discovery of the Proton

Ernest Rutherford later demonstrated that the positively charged particle inside the nucleus is the **proton**.

### Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|--------|
| Symbol | p⁺ |
| Charge | +1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C |
| Mass | 1.673 × 10⁻²⁷ kg |

The number of protons determines the identity of an element.

For example:

| Element | Protons |
|----------|---------|
| Hydrogen | 1 |
| Helium | 2 |
| Carbon | 6 |
| Uranium | 92 |

---

# 10. Discovery of the Neutron (1932)

Although protons explained the positive charge of the nucleus, they could not explain its mass.

British physicist **James Chadwick** discovered another particle:

> **The neutron**

### Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|--------|
| Symbol | n |
| Charge | 0 |
| Mass | 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg |

The neutron was one of the most important discoveries in nuclear physics because:

- It stabilizes the nucleus.
- It can penetrate the nucleus easily.
- It initiates nuclear fission.

Without the neutron, nuclear reactors would not be possible.

---

# 11. Isotopes

Atoms of the same element always have the same number of protons.

However, they may contain different numbers of neutrons.

These variants are called **isotopes**.

Example:

| Isotope | Protons | Neutrons |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Carbon-12 | 6 | 6 |
| Carbon-13 | 6 | 7 |
| Carbon-14 | 6 | 8 |

All three are carbon because each contains **6 protons**.

---

# 12. Nuclear Binding Energy

The nucleus contains many positively charged protons.

Since like charges repel, the nucleus should fly apart.

Why doesn't it?

Because of the **Strong Nuclear Force**.

This force binds protons and neutrons together.

The energy required to completely separate all nucleons is called the **binding energy**.

Higher binding energy means a more stable nucleus.

---

# 13. Mass Defect

When nucleons combine to form a nucleus,

the total mass of the nucleus is slightly **less** than the sum of the individual masses.

This missing mass is called the **mass defect**.

\[
\Delta m = Zm_p + Nm_n - M_{nucleus}
\]

where:

- \(Z\) = Number of protons
- \(N\) = Number of neutrons
- \(M_{nucleus}\) = Measured nuclear mass

The missing mass is converted into binding energy.

---

# 14. Einstein's Mass-Energy Equivalence

In 1905, Albert Einstein proposed one of the most famous equations in physics:

\[
E = mc^2
\]

where:

- \(E\) = Energy
- \(m\) = Mass
- \(c\) = Speed of light

This equation showed that:

- Mass can be converted into energy.
- Energy can be converted into mass.

The energy released in nuclear reactions originates from this mass-to-energy conversion.

This principle forms the basis of:

- Nuclear reactors
- Nuclear fusion
- Nuclear weapons

---

# 15. Importance for Nuclear Engineering

The discoveries made between 1897 and 1932 transformed physics forever.

They enabled scientists to understand:

- Atomic structure
- Nuclear stability
- Radioactivity
- Nuclear reactions
- Chain reactions
- Nuclear fission

These discoveries directly led to the development of the first nuclear reactor.

# 17. Key Takeaways

- The electron was the first subatomic particle discovered.
- Rutherford showed that the atom contains a tiny, dense nucleus.
- Bohr explained atomic stability using quantized energy levels.
- The proton determines the identity of an element.
- The neutron stabilizes the nucleus and enables nuclear fission.
- Isotopes differ in the number of neutrons.
- Mass defect gives rise to nuclear binding energy.
- Einstein's equation \(E = mc^2\) explains the enormous energy released in nuclear reactions.
